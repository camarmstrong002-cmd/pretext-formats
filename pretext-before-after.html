The first ever banner ad: why it worked and what it started

On 27 October 1994, AT&T launched what became the web's first banner ad on HotWired. Thirty years later, we are still living in the rectangle it created.
By The Publisher
Thu 12 Dec 2013 · Updated 27 Oct 2024

On October 27, 1994, the world of advertising was forever transformed by a small graphic bearing the words "Have you ever clicked your mouse right here? You will" in a rainbow font. The age of banner ads had officially begun on HotWired, the digital offshoot of Wired magazine.

HotWired was a digital publication that needed revenue. They devised a plan to sell space to advertisers. They called these "banner ads."

The importance of integrated marketing

For two years prior, AT&T had run a campaign focused on futuristic technological wonders. All ended with the same promise: "You will."

AT&T's campaign represented about $50m in annual spending on TV, radio and print. Consumer awareness ran high.

The first banner went live on hotwired.com on 27 October, 1994.

The importance of experiences over messages

AT&T paid HotWired $30,000 for three months. The click-through rate was an extraordinary 44 per cent — almost fictional by today's standards.

All the promises in the You Will campaign have since come true. But in 1994, the message seemed fantastical.

"The click-through rate was an extraordinary 44% — almost fictional by today's standards, where the average banner CTR hovers around 0.1%"

The birth of an industry

The banner ad concept spread rapidly. Publishers sought advertisers to lease banner space.

WebConnect began helping clients target ideal consumers. They introduced frequency capping.

In 1996, Doubleclick emerged, giving rise to the cost-per-impression model.

Thirty years of the rectangle

Three decades on, the ad is still a rectangle. It still interrupts.

Banner blindness is the rational response to thirty years of rectangular interruption. The question is whether the rectangle is the right shape at all.
